David Ogilvy

Ogilvy believed that every advertisement is a long-term investment in the brand personality. He argued for treating the consumer with intelligence and relying on research to build a consistent image year after year, as people get bored of a campaign long before the customer does. "The customer is not a moron; she's your wife," he famously stated. Every advertisement must be a long-term investment in the brand personality. He argued against "one-off" creative stunts, believing that consistent imagery over decades—rather than years—builds the most profitable market share. View on creativity: For him, creativity is in the precision of the promise, the clarity of the argument, and the elegance of the execution—not in cleverness for its own sake. He distrusted obscurity and “artiness” that got in the way of persuasion.

He believed great advertising is a fusion of art and insight—emotionally truthful, surprising, and respectful of the audience’s intelligence. He rejected formulaic, hard-sell approaches and pushed for simple, witty, and honest communication that reveals a human truth about the product or category. The Creative Revolution and Integrated Teams. He believed in the power of originality, wit, and taste, arguing that an ad's execution was just as important as its message. He championed the "little guy" in campaigns and proved that good taste, creativity, and honesty could sell better than loud, repetitive promises. Advertising is an art of persuasion, not a science. He pioneered the "Creative Team" (pairing a copywriter with an art director), believing that art and copy must be integrated as a single unit to create impact. He believes the truth is the most powerful element in advertising; if a product doesn't have an advantage, he warns that no amount of skill can invent one.
